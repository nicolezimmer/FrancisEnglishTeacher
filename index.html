About
Conditionals

Conditionals, or “if clauses”, are a type of sentence that lets us express in some cases how one thing depends on another. These can be used both in a real or an imaginary case, and are formed by these two clauses:

  1. The Condition (or if clause)
    It starts with if, or a similar word like “unless” or “when” and holds the condition of the sentence.
  2. The result (or main statement)
    Is the result of the condition of the sentence.


Types

There are 4 types of conditionals:

This conditional is used to talk about facts. Both in the first and second clause, the verb goes in the present tense. Its formula is: Present Simple + Present Simple

Example: If you heat the water at 100 degrees Celsius, it boils.

This conditional is used to refer to a future situation we think it's possible. The conditional clause goes in the present tense, while the result goes in simple future. Its formula is: Present Tense + Simple Future.

Example: If you study for your exam, you are going to get a good result.

This conditional is used to talk about an unlikely or unreal situation. The conditional clause goes in simple past, plus a modal verb in the middle, while the result goes in infinite. Its formula is: Past Simple + Would + Infinite.

Example: If we had the opportunity to travel more, I would not complain.

This conditional is used to talk about an imaginary situation that did not happen. The conditional clause goes in past perfect, plus a modal verb in the middle, while the result goes in past participle. Its formula is: Past Perfect + Would Have + Past

Example: If I had studied hard, I would have passed my exams.
